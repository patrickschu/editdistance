<Robert Daborne to Henslowe (1613),
<The Alleyn Papers, ed. Collier, p. 66.

$I    Mr. Hinchlow, I hav ever since I saw y=u= kept my bed, being
$I so lame that I cannot stand. I pray, S=r=, goe forward with
$I that reasonable bargayn for the Bellman; we will hav but twelv
$I pownds and the overplus of the second day, whearof I hav had
$I ten shillings, and desyre but twenty shillings more, till y=u= hav
$I 3 sheets of my papers. Good S=r=, consyder how for y=r= sake I
$I have put my self out of the assured way to get mony, and from
$I twenty pounds a play am come to twelv; thearfor in my ex_tremyty
$I forsake me not, as y=u= shall ever command me. My
$I wif can acquaynt y=u= how infinite great my occation is, and this
$I shall be sufficient for the receipt, till I come to set my hand to
$I your booke.
$I                               yo=r= at comand,
$I                                          ROB. DABORNE.
$I    Aug. 3, 1613.
$I Lent M=r= Daborne upon this not the 32 of
$I    Auguste in earnest of a playe called
$I    the bellman of London, xxs.
